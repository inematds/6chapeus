# Catalog of 10 divergent frameworks

Each framework cuts the problem along a distinct dimension. The art is in picking 3 that combine well, not in using all of them.

## A — Inversion

Instead of asking how to achieve success, identify what would guarantee failure. List the failure modes and design the solution that avoids them.

**Question:** "What would we have to do to guarantee that this fails?"
**Use for:** launches, risk prevention, commercial proposals.

---

## B — Constraint shock

Push constraints to extremes to reveal invisible assumptions: 1/10× of the budget, 100× of the budget, 24 hours, 5 years, without your usual stack. What survives is the core.

**Question:** "What if we had 1/10 of the budget? What if we had 100x?"
**Use for:** revealing assumptions, design under resource scarcity, exploring ceilings.

---

## C — Cross-domain transfer

Import solutions from nature, hospitality, healthcare, military, elite sports, psychology. Describe the STRUCTURE of the problem (not the content) and look for problems with identical structure already solved in another sector.

**Question:** "How would a hospital solve this? An F1 team? A restaurant?"
**Use for:** strategy, service design, process innovation.

---

## D — First principles

Decompose down to irreducible truths (physical, logical, economic, regulatory laws). Rebuild bottom-up, ignoring existing solutions.

**Question:** "What are the irreducible truths here? What's left if I eliminate all conventions?"
**Use for:** pricing, architecture, process design.

---

## E — SCAMPER

Seven transformation operators applied to the current solution:
- **S**ubstitute — which component can I replace?
- **C**ombine — what can I merge?
- **A**dapt — what from another context can I adapt?
- **M**odify — what can I enlarge, reduce, change?
- **P**ut to other uses — what else is it good for?
- **E**liminate — what can I remove?
- **R**eorder — what happens if I change the order?

**Use for:** incremental improvement, productization, offer refactoring.

---

## F — TRIZ (Contradiction)

Identify the central trade-off: improving A makes B worse. Apply separation:
- In **time** (A now, B later)
- In **space** (A here, B there)
- In **conditions** (A when X, B when Y)
- In **system level** (A at the part, B at the whole)

The contradiction dissolves instead of being chosen.

**Question:** "What's the trade-off everyone assumes is inevitable? How do I separate it?"
**Use for:** strategic dilemmas, product design, trade-offs.

---

## G — Random concept injection

Inject a random concept unrelated to the problem (hose, opera, hurricane, lighthouse, bakery, submarine). Force 2-3 connections. The 3rd or 4th connection is usually where the lateral insight appears.

**Question:** "What does [random concept] have to do with my problem?"
**Use for:** creative block, naming, messaging angles.

---

## H — Stakeholder swap

Adopt radically different perspectives:
- Aggressive competitor with 10× the budget
- Untrained intern on day one
- $60k/project consultant
- An AI from 2030
- A 10-year-old
- The worst possible customer
- GDPR / EU AI Act regulator
- Early-stage investor

**Question:** "How does [person X] see this? What would they do?"
**Use for:** revealing blind spots, proposal stress-test, gaps.

---

## I — Time-horizon shift

The time horizon changes the decision criteria:
- Which decision will make you glad 10 years from now?
- Which one will embarrass you?
- If it had to last 50 years, what version would it be?
- What if it only mattered for 2 weeks?

**Question:** "Is this still a good idea at [horizon]?"
**Use for:** career decisions, brand, long-term architecture.

---

## J — Radical elimination

Sometimes the best solution to a problem is to eliminate the problem:
- What if you do nothing?
- Does this already exist and you don't know it?
- Can the problem be eliminated rather than solved?
- What parts are optional and no one has questioned?
- Is it your problem or an inherited one?

**Question:** "Do I really need to solve this?"
**Use for:** project planning, scope audit, before starting.

---

## How to choose 3 frameworks

1. **Technical problem:** D (First principles) + F (TRIZ) + B (Constraint shock)
2. **Strategic problem:** A (Inversion) + C (Cross-domain) + I (Time-horizon)
3. **Creative problem:** G (Random concept) + E (SCAMPER) + H (Stakeholder)
4. **Scope / prioritization problem:** J (Elimination) + B (Constraint shock) + D (First principles)
5. **Interpersonal problem:** H (Stakeholder) + A (Inversion) + I (Time-horizon)

These combinations are suggestive. If a framework doesn't produce anything interesting, swap it. If you're struggling to reach 5 alternatives, add a 4th framework.
